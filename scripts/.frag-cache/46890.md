**Hawas for Him** by **Rasasi** is a Aromatic Aquatic fragrance for men. **Hawas for Him** was launched in 2015. Top notes are Apple, Bergamot, Lemon and Cinnamon; middle notes are Watery Notes, Plum, Orange Blossom and Cardamon; base notes are Ambergris, Musk, Patchouli and Driftwood.

Hawas for Him blends cinnamon, bergamot, orange blossom, grey amber and sandalwood to create an aquatic scent designed to embody masculine strength and vigour. This fresh elegant woody perfume combines the premium Italian citrus and the fruity freshness of pear and pineapple. The heart of this modern fougere is based on aquatic elements combined with melon and violet. It is a mix of sandalwood, cedar wood, musk with generous ambergris. The fragrance is inflected with fresh marine aromatic notes that melt into amber woods. The fragrance captures the adventurous, exploratory and evocative spirit of the modern man. The contemporary notes of this chic fragrance come with an attitude to match. The clear grey bottle has a python wrapped around the stopper while a textured python on the pack captures the sensuality of the species with a flamboyance redolent of haute couture.

 The bottle design is also really nice in my opinion. The heavy cap and simple design stand out, especially compared to many other Middle Eastern fragrances that can be a bit too flashy and extravagant. This one keeps it simple and looks appealing.

 Also, a small note: if you overspray, you become a hazard. Three sprays are more than enough to smell great.

 Overall, the experience is great, and I would recommend this fragrance as a blind buy or even as your first fragrance. It does exactly what it's supposed to do, and it does it very well. Good job to Rassasi for this release!

For the Culture 

 For all of the comparisons to Invictus, and its various sequels scents, Hawas still manages to retain its individuality, and uniqueness as a sweet, spicy, musky aquatic. This remains the case even with comparisons to Acqua Di Gio Absolu — which I have beside me as I write this.

 To be fair: all of the above mentioned fragrances fall within the same genre of men’s fragrance, so to find commonalities amongst them is no different than discovering similarities between, say, vehicles, homes, cinematography or any other commodity that one desires to fulfill a particular purpose. Sport sedans are all four-door, spirited vehicles with excellent handling and suspension. Beach homes are designed to capitalize on the fact the home is on the beach, and romantic comedies are romantic, and comedic. Often I find the criticism of likeness among fragrance options to be both unwarranted, and generally exaggerated by the majority of aficionados — not to mention unhelpful.

 Plus, the rumor of reformulation is completely overblown. I’ve owned a bottle of Hawas each year since its inception. They all perform, and smell virtually the same. Moreover, this isn’t the sort of fragrance one easily becomes anosmic to. Simply put: the shit is strong. 

 On the opening, you will receive a very sweet, dense, spicy, and vaguely aquatic impression due to the apple, plum, cinnamon, and lemon. The musk, patchouli, and ambergris more or less are there, but won’t play vigorously until the fragrance begins to dry. Oddly enough, Hawas (throughout its peculiarly long lifespan for an aquatic) never truly loses all of its primary notes until well near the ten hour mark. This offers a nice bit of complexity, as the scent will transition to various phases depending on skin chemistry, and ambient temperature. 

 Hawas also has a quality which you do not find amongst Absolu, or Invictus, Wanted, or even the various Armaf renditions of the sweet, musky aquatics: Hawas has a sort of dense, nearly dirtiness to it somehow. The ambergris lends a very slight saltiness to the overall composition that offers an accord that is both pleasant somehow, while being distinctive enough to differentiate Hawas from the aforementioned. That is to say Hawas possesses enough varying qualities to merit a test if you find yourself wanting something relatively unique, but similar to the available plethora. 

 It also might be worth noting that due to the cardamom, and cinnamon, Hawas can also be worn in colder months without much hassle, too. As I had mentioned earlier, Hawas is quite dense. In cold, it performs very well, although the scent itself begins to carry less of the salty ambergris in lieu of a heightened sweetness in conjunction with spice that comes across as candy or beverage-like.

 If you like Absolu, but want more complexity or depth, try Hawas. If you enjoy Invictus, but perhaps would like a fragrance that is similar, but with a noticeable twist, try Hawas. If you favor Wanted, but desire a departure from your norm, try Hawas. If you have within your collection enough of this specific genre, you can absolutely skip Hawas, and miss nothing whatsoever. The fragrance is close enough to the median of the scent type that it’s nothing especially ingenious. But, it is very good, and my personal favorite of the bunch. 

 Insofar as longevity, and projection: the scent is not leaving your body unless accompanied by a shower, and you should receive approximately three hours of more than arm’s length projection. Once it mellows, it sits at maybe half that distance, but stays there densely for many hours. 

 The dry down, for most of the fragrance is simply the mid, but waning. You’ll know the scent is dying when you mostly smell ambergris, patchouli, and musk. But, by that time it will more than probably be the next day. 

 9/10. Easily one of my favorite fragrances. 

 Lastly, I just want to wish you all well. Times appear perilous, but I assure you, just how all good things must come to an end, so too must the bad. If nothing else, know that I care. See you around, superstar. Be easy.
