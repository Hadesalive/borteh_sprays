**Mukhalat Malaki** by **Swiss Arabian** is a Oriental Woody fragrance for men. The fragrance features Agarwood (Oud), Turkish Rose Oil, Saffron, Woodsy Notes, Amber, Musk and Sandalwood.

"Mukhalat Malaki is a rich, sophisticated scent created for the pleasure of Arabian nobility. The fragrance is concocted using traditional Arabic perfume oils that blend together to deliver a powerfully prolonged moment of fragrant extravagance." - a note from the brand.

There is nothing particularly special or noteworthy here, just that it’s beautiful. There are four primary accords that, because this is a straight Arabian perfume oil, do not act in the typical French manner of top, heart, base with dry downs as such. There is a bit of that, but it’s minimal. Most notably in reference to that typical structure Western noses are accustomed to is the saffron that opens the perfume. The saffron is beautifully hay-like, a sweet and dried grass, with a touch of florals. It dispenses rather quickly (about an hour; remember this is a perfume oil) to reveal the stars of Mukhalat Malaki: rose, oud, and amber. The rose is pink, sweet, and powdery, and is kept sort of in the background, like a harmony singer of a three-part choral group. Its harmony line comes in and out of focus throughout the nearly twelve hour life of the perfume depending upon how thickly you put it on. The star carrying the primary vocal line is oud. The oud in MM lacks a lot of the funky, fungal, turned-fruit type aspects and relishes in the barnyard animalics. It carries the hay like qualities of the saffron into the rest of the perfume’s life, while also providing the stable animal fecal, woody, and dirty notes we’ve come to expect and love of oud. The amber accord is a seamless deadlock of balsams, to my nose sandalwood and labdanum, playing the role of rhythmic bass, adding a smooth depth, sweetness, and roundness to the whole of the perfume. It works to keep the oud in check, giving the whole a regal and elegant feel. 

 There are hundreds if not thousands of these perfumes out there; this template has been a staple in Arabia for centuries. It’s far from new, and far from being the best, but I love it all the same. I’ve worn it for special occasions throughout the year - including the summer, because you can. It is remarkably well behaved, even in summer heat (just go light on the application). It spends most of its time as a strong skin scent, so it won’t blow away you or others around you. If you’re looking for an inexpensive and well-done example of this very old genre, I’d point to this more often than not.
