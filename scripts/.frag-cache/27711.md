 Introduction:

 TN started out as an easy reach for warmer weather. However, it's mid-November now and I'm still rockin' it as I brush the snow from my car! Despite the seasonal votes near the top of the page, TN performs strongly for me in cold weather. I will continue pushing the seasonal boundaries of this as far as I can, both day and evening. 

 I like it so very much that I've got a bottle of it's inspiration—Creed GIT on order, along with a similar frag by the same nose that created GIT—Davidoff Cool Water, and another clone—Al Haramain Amber Oud Carbon. When they all get here and I've had an opportunity to compare them all, I'll do so in the GIT review. 

 TN right from the box was gorgeous, albeit slightly rough in the opening, but after owning it a few months it has matured and stabilized marvelously. 

 I performed this test at home, in quiet solitude, and was really paying close attention to how this fragrance unfolds. TN develops so naturally and seamlessly that when I'm out and about, or have other competing stimuli, I am scarcely aware of the details related below, just the exquisite overall experience. As I was writing this, the description sort of organically developed into a ballroom metaphor, so I ran with it.

 Opening: 

 The citrus guests arrive early as the orchestra tunes up and begins to play. The lavender and violet florals arrive on their heels and the dance begins quietly, with a little bit of citrus sparkle. Then it just keeps getting better as more guests arrive.

 Drydown: 

 In about 20 minutes a troop of iris appear and the citruses fade some, but not completely. They sit down around the perimeter to watch and chat softly but do not leave. The iris check their cloaks, powder their noses and join the dance of the flowers that the violet and lavender are still doing, making it more complex and interesting. 

 After about 90 minutes the citrus have finished their drinks, collect their wraps and vanish quietly. Meanwhile, the ambergris and sandalwood have entered the room, subtly but powerfully. These new guests don't dominate the dance, but their presence strengthens and renews the efforts of the others. The ballroom becomes darker and warmer, and the tempo and intensity of the dance slowly increases. The ballet gradually becomes more complex and more sensual, all the while with reserve and control.

 After about three hours the tempo and intensity have reached their apex and begin to subside. One by one the dancers leave the room, slowly and unobtrusively. At about six hours all the guests have gone, the orchestra packs up, and all that remains is a beautiful memory in the form of a skin scent.

 If you want the dance to last all night... carry a travel spray and apply some more at about 3-4 hours.

 Gender:

 Again, I'm older and often consider floral fragrances to be feminine. Tres Nuit? Au contraire! Perfumery and alchemy are both occult practices IMO. I don't understand how Armaf took a boatload of iris, lavender, and violet and created something unmistakably masculine from them. It's not too sweet, and it ain't girlie at all.

 Tres Nuit a masterpiece of perfumery. Kudos to Armaf on this one.

 Bottom Line:

 Scent:--------10/10

 Longevity:-----7/10

 Sillage:---------9/10

 Value:---------10/10

 Edit: Comparison to GIT

 Although Tres Nuit is a pretty good clone of GIT, and they do share many characteristics, I find them a little different. GIT is sharper and has more sparkle in the opening, while TN is smoother. In the drydown Tres Nuit evolves into something more deeply smooth, powdery and floral than the ozonic/aquatic direction that GIT takes. The iris, violet and lavender notes of TN lead you down a darker garden path than the trail through a green sunlit meadow bordering the forest and ocean that GIT will take you. Both of these fragrances are absolutely exquisite in their own way, but if I had to make a choice between the two... I'd wear the Armaf Tres Nuit. Better yet, since I have both, I'll wear GIT in the morning and the TN in the evening.
