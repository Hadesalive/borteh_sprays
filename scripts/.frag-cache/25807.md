 Some people say Raghba and Artisan Ethnique are similar. And while there are similarities between the two fragrances, there are distinct differences as well especially in the base notes, or dry-downs.

 I like Raghba but I greatly prefer Artisan Ethnique. AE is more complex, more interesting.

Stepping into the world of Middle Eastern fragrances, I wasn't quite sure what to expect. Raghba, one of the first strictly Middle Eastern fragrances that wasn't a clone, immediately introduced me to a world of bold, resinous sweetness, smoky warmth, and unexpected nuances. It has since set a standard for what I seek in this style. My bottle is an older, pre-aged one, which may have influenced my experience compared to those who review it in its freshest state.

 The dominant theme is burnt sugar blended with warm, ambery vanilla and a touch of incense. Despite being a creamy scent, the opening has a somewhat metallic edge, which I personally find an intriguing addition — a sharp contrast to its otherwise smooth texture. Perhaps this explains why some people perceive a saliva-like nuance — without a trained nose, I might have made a similar association.

 The scent does not undergo any major transformation; what emerges in the first few minutes largely defines the entire wear, though subtle shifts in balance occur. The drydown introduces mineral, salty nuances, resembling a woody oud infused with liquor. The overall impression carries a certain nostalgia, reminiscent of a sweet dessert with sticky, stretchy caramel poured over charred wood. It also evokes the cozy imagery of a cabin in the woods by a crackling fireplace.

 Performance-wise, it boasts very good longevity with above-average projection, though it is not a powerhouse. It is one of the most universally wearable and unisex fragrances, fitting seamlessly for anyone, anywhere, in any season. In summer, its animalic facets become more pronounced, but it finds its true rhythm in cooler months when it settles beautifully into crisp air.

 I find it misplaced when people label this an 'unsafe' blind buy. If you're venturing into this style of perfumery, you likely have an idea of what to expect. Complaining about Raghba feels like buying a super-hot pepper and being surprised when it burns.

 For those who enjoy layering, Raghba serves as a versatile base — linear enough to complement other scents while still maintaining its distinct identity. For me, it remains a cornerstone of Middle Eastern perfumery — a timeless composition that perfectly captures the essence of this genre.
